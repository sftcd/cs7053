News Stories

I usually update this sporadically during the run-time of the module with links to stories that I might want to reference in class.

2024

  • A nice side-channel description of a near-practical attack affecting Yubikeys
  • A Wired article showing in nice but easily understood detail the awfulness of advertising based on real time bidding.
  • NDSS paper on attacking fingerprint authentication by listening to the sound of swipes on screens - Nice! (but be interested in seeing it reproduced).
  • NSA and other bits of US DoD admit to buying tracking data from brokers. El reg article, and in case it ever gets lost a local copy of the letters..
  • Another case of facial recognition technology (FRT) seeming to produce a bad outcome, from the Guardian.
  • Cute paper on an impractical but interesting leak Imaging privacy threats from an ambient light sensor.
  • A claim of a break in the privacy of Apple airplay exchanges by a Chinese "state-backed institution". No real detail as yet, but interesting mention of using rainbow tables to reverse some kind of identifier hashing/obfuscation.

2023

  • Nice paper about SSH host-key RSA private key recovery in the face of faults: https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1711.pdf.
  • Yet another radio-frequency side-channel report
  • Car privacy is bad, says Mozilla.
  • Some researchers caught ETSI out in backdooring TETRA crypto. For some reason an ETSI staffer decided to do a car-crash interview on the topic.There're always singple point of failure stories, but this one's interesting - a satellite SPOF added to a nicely redundant system, that breaks farms in Australia!
  • Interesting attack against a vehicle CAN bus enabling car theft via wiring within headlights.
  • A fairly bad sounding multi-year attack on hoster GoDaddy and their customers. Things like that are bad.
  • Another story about supposedly "smart" devices not bring smart.
  • Another reason to not leave MD5 code hanging about: it enables bad hits in certificate caches.
  • An audit of passwords found: "the auditors cracked 18,174—or 21 percent—of the 85,944 cryptographic hashes they tested; 288 of the affected accounts had elevated privileges, and 362 of them belonged to senior government employees. In the first 90 minutes of testing, auditors cracked the hashes for 16 percent of the department’s user accounts" - good to see some recent numbers for that. The passwords were from active directory and the article mentions NTLM, which historically has been speedy to crack, but that level of detail isn't clear. (the underlying audit report)
  • Leaked android OEM private keys that have yet to be replaced? Odd.
  • Really a late-2022 story: Another CA booted from the Mozilla root program, this time for possible connections to a spyware company.

2022

  • A nice paper describing how passive measurement of TLS1.2 and earlier handshakes can spot occasional faulty signatures that can expose a private key.
  • A good report on a FB phishing/bot campaign that also does some DDoS (albeit small scale).
  • Random improvements: /dev/random and /dev/urandom merged in Linux kernel.
  • Hasn't made much news, but this preprint from Ward Beullens describes a key recovery attack on the NIST PQ-competition signature-algorithm finalist Rainbow. Assuming that result holds, (and it seems to be accepted by the inventors), it's a good lesson about new algorithms.
  • Some interesting vulnerabilities in UPS devices (el reg report, tech report, local copy)
  • In what may be good news, the Belgian DPA has agreed with the Irish Council of Civil Liberties that the so-called "consent" framework underlying a lot of real time bidding (RTB) on the web is not compatible with the GPDR. That'll probably run and run, but it's a hopeful sign IMO.
  • Publisher (Springer) adding per-download unique metadata to PDFs, presumably for tracking or traitor tracing. I added remove-pdf-metadata.sh as a result and cleaned the PDFs in this repo.
  • Log4j zero day was a real doozy - I know a bunch of people who had to spend lots of time mitigating that. (It was discovered in Nov 2021 but I only added it here in Jan 2022:-)
  • El Reg story on decomposition and attribution of a sophisticated APT attacker tool. (56-page report: original link, local copy).

2021

  • Nice fingerprint story.
  • It's quite old, but I've only read it now, so worth nothing that this explanation of Meltown is the best I've seen, so worth a read. (It's from the founder of the rPi foundation, and is really well written.)
  • We often forget how we depend on the kindness of strangers: a reminder of that when a sysadmin sells access to customers email accounts.
  • Another WebPKI CA in trouble: the El reg article is ok, but the real meat is in the Mozilla wiki and the links from there.
  • Two recent interesting "tracking" stories - one from the NY Times on how AD IDs allow tracking of people who were around the US Capitol building on Jan 6th, and another from Bellingcat describing how they claim to have determined who likely carried out a recent poisoning. When reading these, try consider how your opinion may change if you were sympathetic to those being tracked.
